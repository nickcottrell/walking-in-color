Part Two: The Dog's Computation

Here’s where I must be careful, for the paper on ye dogs does more than it claims.

Now, mark ye down that scholars at Nagaoka University of Technology employed 3D X-ray CT scanning and fluid dynamics simulation to model the airflow within a dog's nasal cavity. What they found was that the nose isn’t just one sensing organ; it’s three systems all working together. A fast route handles breathing by gas exchange, a slow one manages humidification and temperature, and a turbulent route, through a labyrinthine system of scroll-like structures called turbinates, is dedicated to odor collection.

Three routes. Three systems. Each doing its task simultaneously.

But here be the part where they don’t say, and I’m extrapolating from it—mark ye well: dogs have approximately 811 olfactory receptor genes. Humans only have around 398. But these aren’t just more of the same; each receptor works in a combinatorial fashion. They respond to multiple molecular compounds, and the pattern of which receptors fire together—that combination—is what creates this dimensional space.

This isn’t simply 811 parallel channels. It’s a system where interference patterns between receptor activations create a space with millions of addressable coordinates.

Your brain does something similar with color. You don’t have three separate systems each reporting a number independently. You have three cone types whose relative outputs are compared by opponent-processing neurons that generate the full perceptual color space from the ratios between them. It’s interference all the way down.

But this dog is running this combinatorial computation in a space orders of magnitude larger than anything ye can access visually. The result be something that sounds almost supernatural when you say it plainly: a dog can smell a fire hydrant and read, in order, every dog that's interacted with that spot—including their health status, their emotional state at the time, and roughly how recent each visit was. A tamper-proof chain of molecular cause and effect, written in scent, readable in real time.

That’s not just a sensitive nose. That’s a biological computation system running vector geometry across hundreds of dimensions simultaneously.

Jack Jack isn’t sniffing the hedge. He is running a query.

Part Three: The Color Ye Can't See

Ahoy, me hearty! I want ye to feel somethin’ before I tell ye bout them hummingbirds. Look at what ye be lookin' at right now. Ye perceive color through three sorts o' cones in yer retina—long, medium, and short wavelengths. Everything ye’ve ever seen—every painting, every sunset, every face ye love—is constructed from three channels of information, mixed in varying proportions, interpreted by yer trichromatic visual cortex.

Now, think on this: about 30% of the color info present in bird plumage, and approximately 35% in flowering plants, falls outside the space yer three cones can access. It’s not that ye see it differently or that it looks muted or simplified to ye. No, it simply don’t exist in yer experience. The signal is there, but your sensors lack the channel.

At Princeton, researchers trained wild hummingbirds in field experiments in the Rocky Mountains, where they associated colors with sucrose rewards. They confirmed that these feathered fellows can discriminate nonspectral colors—colors like UV+red and UV+green—that are literally unimaginable to a trichromat. Not hard to see, not subtle. Unimaginable! There’s no way for ye to know what UV+red looks like to a hummingbird because you don’t have the receptor that would generate the experience.

A fourth channel doesn’t just add more color; it adds a dimension. The geometry of the bird's color space is a tetrahedron, while yours is a triangle. The colors at the center of their tetrahedron—generated by the interaction of four non-adjacent cone types—are not on yer map. Ye are walking through a world where roughly a third of the color signal is inaccessible to ye.

Part Four: What the Engineers Found

Here be strange waters, me hearty.

At Quanzhou Normal University, there be a scholar who penned a treatise on visible light communication—sailing the seas of data through beams of light rather than waves. His ship uses laser diodes, three in number—red at 650nm, green at 530nm, and blue at 450nm—along with polarization multiplexing, to convey six channels of 10 gigabits per second each over a span of 500 meters.

Aye, 60 gigabits per second through three colors and two states o' polarization. Strange, but true.

Why the choice o’ three? Not because the engineer was thinking 'bout human eyes or hummingbirds or dog noses. No, it’s because three spectrally separated wavelengths be orthogonal—they don't interfere with one another, corrupting nary a bit of the signal. They can sail together through the same medium and part clean at the other end, as if they occupy different parts o' the electromagnetic spectrum.

The info stays coherent on each channel because the channels are independent—like three ships in formation, they generate a space larger than any one could alone while keeping their signals intact under pressure.

This scholar be a clever man, arriving at this same architecture that evolution built into ye own eyes, yer dog’s nose, and a hummingbird’s eye. Not three o’ the same thing, but three orthogonal things that together make a space greater than each on its own, while still maintaining signal integrity.

I usually stop walkin' here. Jack Jack be pleased to take his night watch.

What this scholar calls wavelength division multiplexing, evolution calls a sensory system. What he terms orthogonal channels, yer visual cortex calls opponent processing. And what the scholar notes as signal integrity under compression? Why, yer dog would call it smellin’ last Tuesday.

The principle be same—different substrate, but the same solution. Both biological evolution and human engineering independently findin' this same answer suggests it’s not a clever trick. It's a fundamental property of how we efficiently generate high-dimensional information space from a limited medium.

Now, there be many who would scoff at such notions, but ye must admit, there be something to be said for the wisdom o’ nature and the ingenuity o’ man. Both workin' towards a common end in their own unique ways, like two ships sailin' on different routes but reachin' the same port.

So here we stand, with this principle showin' us that sometimes, the simplest answer be the most profound. And as I walk my watch, I can't help but wonder what other secrets these waves of light might hold, waitin' to be discovered by those who seek them out.

Part Five: The Mantis Shrimp Problem

Aye, there be twelve t' sixteen types o' photoreceptors in the mantis shrimp.

Now ye hear the tale they tell: that makes it see colors we cannae fathom, does it not? Sixteen dimensions of color! It must have some transcendent experience!

But listen to me now. That’s probably wrong, and the truth be more interesting than any story.

Research on how the mantis shrimp distinguishes colors suggests they’re not so much better at seeing shades as humans are. They don’t seem to use sixteen channels for a richer, continuous color experience like some fancy tetrachromat might do. Instead, they appear to run parallel categories—more like sorting into bins quickly, “Which category is this stimulus in, and can I tell ye that fast?”

They’re not makin’ maps with sixteen dimensions. They’re more like cross-referencing a set o' tables at once.

Now here’s where it gets interesting: what the mantis shrimp has evolved might be computational efficiency over richer perception. Sixteen parallel channels each spitting out categorical answers in one swoop is faster than runnin’ through a complex, high-dimensional space. Ye trade nuance for speed. Ye give up on fine detail for throughput.

Back to Jack Jack, runnin' four simultaneous processes across multiple senses. And there I am thinkin’ of the Fudan researchers showin’ that human RGB processing is sequential—red first, then green, and blue last. Wonderin’ if we made that tradeoff. High-dimensional continuous color space givin’ us extraordinary nuance. We pay a price in time, though.

The mantis shrimp runs parallel categorical channels, payin’ for it in resolution but gainin’ speed.

Jack Jack’s dog runs hundreds o' olfactory dimensions, payin’ for visual processing to get denser chemical information.

The hummingbird adds a fourth visual channel and gets access to a geometric dimension that reorganizes signal from noise.

Different architectures of parallel channel processing, each correspondin’ to different forms and efficiencies o’ intelligence. Not better or worse—just different computational tradeoffs. Different solutions to the same problem: how do ye generate high-dimensional info with limited biological stuff?

If you could measure the channel density and dimensional capacity of a sensory system—the number o' orthogonal channels, their combinatorial depth, parallel processing architecture—you might be able to estimate the computational envelope of the intelligence runnin’ on it. Not its contents or experience. Just its range.

I’ve been callin’ this thing the Hofstadter unit after Douglass Hofstadter who spent a career tryin’ to understand how strange loops generate consciousness from computation. I don’t know yet if it’s real, but it feels like it should be.
